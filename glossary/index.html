The glossary

What these numbers actually mean

Every town page on this site shows the same set of housing market numbers. None of them are mysterious, but most of them get glossed over or misexplained. If you have been quietly studying the market and want a clear read on what each one really tells you, this is the page. The definitions below are written the way I would explain them to a client over coffee, not the way a textbook would.

Median Sale Price Median List Price Median $/sqft # of Sales New Listings Pending Sales Inventory Months of Supply Median Days on Market Sale-to-List Ratio % Sold Above List % with Price Drops Off Market in 2 Weeks
01

Median Sale Price

Half the homes that closed sold for more than this number, half sold for less. The median is the middle, not the average, which matters in real estate because one $5 million sale will not pull the median sideways the way it would skew an average.

When you see "median sale price up 8% year-over-year," you are seeing where the middle of the market settled, not where one outlier closed. This is the headline number, but it is not the whole story. A town can have a flat median while individual homes are wildly outpacing or trailing the market.

02

Median List Price

The middle asking price of homes that came on the market, before anyone made an offer. Same idea as median sale price, but for what sellers wanted instead of what they got.

The gap between median list and median sale tells you how realistic local pricing has been. When list prices run well above sale prices, sellers are still anchored to last year. When the two are close, the market has caught up to where sellers are.

03

Median $/sqft

Sale price divided by square footage. The reason this matters more than median sale price for comparing different houses is obvious once you see it: a 4,000 square foot colonial and an 1,800 square foot cape are different things, even on the same block.

Price per foot strips out size so you can actually compare apples to apples across homes, blocks, and neighborhoods. If you are weighing two similar homes in different parts of town, this is the number to lean on.

04

# of Sales

How many homes actually closed in the month. Bergen towns range from a few hundred sales (Hackensack, Fort Lee) down to under five (Rockleigh, Teterboro).

Below ten sales, the data starts to wobble. A single luxury closing can move medians by 20% in a small-volume town, and at that point you are not seeing a trend, you are seeing one transaction. On the small towns we hide the misleading data and show a three-month blend instead.

05

New Listings

Homes that came on the market during the period. This is one of the strongest leading indicators in the data.

When new listings spike, supply is loosening and buyers get more choice. When new listings collapse, sellers are sitting on their hands and competition for whatever is available heats up fast. Watching this number against sales tells you whether the market is in flow or in friction.

06

Pending Sales

Homes that went under contract during the period. They have not closed yet and might not for another 30 to 60 days, but they tell you what closings will look like soon.

Strong pending sales now means strong closed sales next month. If pendings drop sharply, expect closed-sale numbers to follow. This is the metric that catches turning points before the median sale price does.

07

Inventory

How many active listings exist on the last day of the period. This is a snapshot, not a flow number.

When inventory grows, homes are sitting longer and the buyer side is gaining leverage. When it shrinks, sellers are in control. Compared against new listings, inventory tells you whether homes are coming on faster than they are selling, or the other way around.

08

Months of Supply

Inventory divided by the current sales pace. If 50 homes are listed and 25 sell per month, that is 2 months of supply. The cleanest single read on whether a market favors buyers or sellers.

Under 4 months is a seller’s market with bidding wars, fast offers, and sellers in control. 4 to 6 months is balanced. Over 6 months is a buyer’s market with patient buyers, motivated sellers, and room to negotiate. Most Bergen towns sit between 2 and 5 most of the year.

09

Median Days on Market

How long the typical home spent on the market before going under contract. Useful, but with one big caveat.

This metric gets noisy in towns with mixed inventory. A tear-down or a stale luxury condo that lingers 200 days will pull the number up even when normal homes are flying off in two weeks. I trust it more for reading trends month to month than for evaluating any single absolute number.

10

Sale-to-List Ratio

The average of (sale price divided by list price) across closed homes. 100% means the typical home sold at asking. 102% means buyers paid 2% over on average, which is competitive. 97% means sellers gave up 3% on average, which is room to negotiate.

This is the metric I quote most often in actual conversations because it answers the question buyers and sellers actually ask: "did people pay asking, or not?" It excludes outliers more than 50% off list, which keeps tear-down sales from pulling it around.

11

% Sold Above List

Share of closed homes that went above asking. A different metric than sale-to-list because it is binary: did they pay over, or did they not.

When 60% of homes go above list, you are writing your strongest offer up front or you are losing. When it is 20%, asking the seller to come down is a reasonable conversation. Pair this with sale-to-list ratio to read negotiation conditions accurately.

12

% with Price Drops

Share of active listings that have reduced their asking price below original. This is a gauge of seller pain.

When a quarter of listings have dropped, plenty of sellers mispriced and are coming back to reality. When it is 5%, sellers are confident and pricing is working out of the gate. A rising price-drop share is often the first crack in a hot market.

13

Off Market in 2 Weeks

Share of homes that went under contract within 14 days of listing. Closely related to months of supply, but more granular for hot stretches.

When over 50% sell in two weeks, the well-priced homes barely give buyers time to schedule a second showing. When it is under 20%, plenty of homes sit long enough for buyers to think, sleep on it, and come back. This is the metric that tells you whether your weekend is going to be panicked or paced.

Reading the data, in context

The numbers tell a story. Your specific situation tells a different one.

These metrics describe what the average is doing. They do not describe what your home, your block, or the house you are watching is doing.
