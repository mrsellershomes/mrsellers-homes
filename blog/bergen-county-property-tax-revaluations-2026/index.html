Bergen County 2026 Property Tax Revaluations: Every Town on the List (and What It Means for Your Bill)

By Tyler Sellers · July 14, 2026

Last updated July 2026. The rate table below uses 2025 certified rates because the 2026 certified rates had not been published at this update. I will refresh every number when the state posts the 2026 table, typically late summer.

Here is the number that should get your attention: 60 of Bergen County's 70 municipalities are resetting property assessments within two years. 36 towns implemented a revaluation or reassessment for tax year 2026, and another 24 are on the county's list for 2027. Both counts come straight from the official lists, the NJ Division of Taxation's 2026 approved list and the Bergen County Board of Taxation.

I sell homes here. This page exists because the tax number on almost every listing in these towns is about to be wrong, and because a genuinely false rumor about one of them keeps landing in my DMs. Every figure below is sourced, and I'll tell you which items are officially confirmed versus reported.

Two terms, defined once. Your assessment is the value the town puts on your property for tax purposes. The levy is the total dollars the town needs to collect from all property owners combined. Your bill is your assessment times the tax rate, and the rate is just the levy divided by the town's total assessments.

Is my town being revalued in 2026?

If you're in one of the 36 towns below, yes, it already happened for tax year 2026. The state and county lists match exactly, all 36 towns, zero discrepancies. If your town isn't here, check the 2027 list further down. 24 more towns are in that pipeline.

How to read the table: the general rate is dollars of tax per $100 of assessed value. The effective rate is your taxes as a percentage of true market value, which makes it the number a buyer can actually use. The last column is that effective rate applied to $1,000,000 of purchase price. Scale it to your price. These are 2025 certified rates, and I'll swap in the 2026 rates the day they publish.

Look at the spread. Effective rates across these 36 towns run from 0.871% in Rockleigh to 2.459% in River Edge. Same county, nearly a 3x difference in what the same purchase price costs you per year.

What's the difference between a revaluation and a reassessment?

Same destination, different driver. A revaluation is a town-wide reappraisal of every property by an outside firm, inspections included, ordered when assessments have drifted too far from market value. A reassessment is the same town-wide reset done by the town's own assessor. Some towns run an annual reassessment program and reset values to market every single year, which is why a few names appear on these lists in back-to-back years.

The key word in all three is town-wide. Everyone resets together. New Jersey's constitution requires every property to be assessed at the same standard of value, and that uniformity requirement matters a lot in the next two sections.

Will my property taxes go up after a revaluation?

Not automatically, and this is the part almost everyone gets wrong. A revaluation doesn't raise a single new dollar for the town. It redistributes the same levy across corrected values. When assessments go up town-wide, the rate comes down.

The Division of Taxation's own worked example makes it concrete. A town needs a $15 million levy on $600 million of total assessments, so the rate is $2.50 per $100. After revaluation, assessments double to $1.2 billion. The rate falls to $1.25. In the Division's words, "the revaluation would not have caused an increase in total taxes to be collected by the municipality. The tax rate would have declined proportionately."

So what moves your individual bill? One thing: whether your value rose more or less than the town-wide average. Rose more than average, your bill goes up. Rose less, it goes down. Rose exactly average, roughly flat.

And here's why your town is on the list in the first place. In six of these 36 towns, Dumont, Ramsey, Ridgefield, River Edge, Rockleigh and South Hackensack, the 2025 effective rate was actually higher than the general rate. That means average assessments there were running above market value. Some owners were quietly overpaying relative to their neighbors. That drift, in both directions, is exactly the problem a reset exists to fix.

Is a Bergen County town really adding a 2% tax on the purchase price?

No. There's a rumor making the rounds that one of these towns is about to start charging buyers 2% of their purchase price in annual property tax. No New Jersey municipality can do that. Property tax here is assessed value times the town's rate, period. Towns have no legal power to tax you on what you paid.

They also can't fake it by reassessing only the people who just bought. The NJ Supreme Court shut that down in Township of West Milford v. Van Decker, 120 N.J. 354 (1990). Reassessing recently sold homes while leaving everyone else alone, the "welcome stranger" pattern, violates the state constitution's uniformity clause. Your assessment cannot be raised just because you paid a high price.

Here's the arithmetic that makes the rumor feel true anyway. Buy at $1,000,000 in a town with a roughly 2% effective rate, and in a reassessment year your new assessment lands near market value, which for you is close to what you just paid. Taxes: about $20,000 a year. That's 2% of your purchase price. It looks like a purchase-price tax. It isn't. It's full market value times the existing rate, applied to every property in town at the same time, including the neighbor who bought in 1998. The difference matters: your number won't jump again just because you were the recent buyer, and it's fully estimable before you offer.

How do I estimate property taxes when buying in a revaluation town?

Take your expected purchase price and multiply by the town's effective tax rate. That's the whole method. $800,000 in Paramus at the 2025 effective rate of 1.434% is about $11,470 a year. The actual 2026 bill gets set by the 2026 certified rate, so treat this as a budgeting number, not a quote.

What you should not use is the tax figure on the listing page. In a revaluation town that number describes the seller's old assessment under the old rate, and both just changed. I've watched buyers rule houses in or out over a listing-page tax number that was already obsolete. Run the effective-rate math instead, or send me the address and I'll run it for you.

One more place taxes show up: the closing table. Property taxes get prorated between buyer and seller by days of ownership, and in a reval year the proration math deserves a second look. I broke down every closing line item, prorations included, in my Bergen County seller closing costs guide.

Can I still appeal my 2026 assessment?

No. The 2026 window is closed, all deadlines have passed, and the county's own site now says so. For the record, the 2026 cycle ran on several tracks: April 1 was the standard statutory deadline, May 1 applied in towns that implemented a revaluation or reassessment, an April 13 date was reported for New Milford and Waldwick, a May 4 extension was reported for a twelve-town group, and Leonia and Wood-Ridge were extended to May 22, with Wood-Ridge confirming that date on the borough's own site.

If you're in a 2026 town and your November tax bill looks wrong relative to your neighbors, that becomes a 2027 appeal conversation. And if you're in the 2027 cohort below, the actionable advice is simple: watch your mail. New assessment notices typically arrive between mid-November and the end of December, and there's an informal hearing option before anything is final. Don't ignore that letter.

Per the county Board of Taxation's list as of January 7, 2026, marked subject to change, 24 towns are slated for 2027: Bergenfield, Cliffside Park, Emerson, Englewood, Fair Lawn, Fort Lee, Franklin Lakes, Garfield, Glen Rock, Hackensack, Harrington Park, Haworth, Hillsdale, Lodi, Mahwah, Midland Park, Montvale, Park Ridge, Ridgewood, River Vale, Rutherford, Tenafly, Upper Saddle River and Wyckoff.

If you own or you're shopping in any of them, everything on this page applies to you one year later. Same mechanics, same rule of thumb, same reason not to panic when the assessment letter shows a much bigger number.

What if my town isn't on either list?

Then your town most likely reset recently. Demarest, New Milford and Wallington all implemented revaluations or reassessments for tax year 2025, per the state's 2025 approved list.

Demarest is worth a closer look, because it's the clearest recent example of everything on this page. A one-shot reset after years of drift produced some of the biggest single-year assessment jumps in the county, in the town with Bergen's highest average residential tax bill. The panic that followed came almost entirely from the assessment-up-equals-bill-up myth debunked above. One year later, the state's equalization table shows Demarest assessed at 101.41% of market value. The reset is done. The bills did not double.

One more pattern worth knowing: many towns appear on these lists in back-to-back years because they run annual reassessment programs that reset values to market every single year. Small yearly adjustments, no shock letters. Will my property taxes go up after a revaluation in NJ?

Not automatically. The tax rate resets downward when assessments rise, because the revaluation itself doesn't change the total dollars the town collects. Your bill rises only if your value went up more than the town-wide average, and it can fall if your value went up less.

No. New Jersey property tax is assessed value times the town's rate, never a percentage of what you paid. In a revaluation town every property resets to market value, and for a recent buyer market value sits near the price paid, which is why the rumor feels true.
