What Attorney Review Actually Means in NJ

By Tyler Sellers · July 11, 2026

You accepted an offer on your house. Congratulations, and also, not so fast. In New Jersey, a signed contract isn't binding yet. Both sides get a built-in escape hatch called attorney review, and it's where more North Jersey deals wobble than anywhere except inspections.

Here's how it actually works.

The 3-day clock

When a Realtor prepares the contract (in Bergen County that's the standard NJ Realtors form), attorney review starts the day after both buyer and seller have the fully signed copy. The window is 3 business days. Saturdays, Sundays, and legal holidays don't count.

During those 3 days, either attorney can "disapprove" the contract. That word sounds dramatic. It isn't. Disapproval is routine: it's how your attorney reopens the contract to fix language, adjust dates, or add protections. The deal isn't dead. It's being negotiated by lawyers instead of agents.

One thing most sellers don't realize: once an attorney disapproves within the window, review effectively stays open until both attorneys conclude it in writing. I've seen "3-day review" run 10 days because both lawyers were trading revisions. Nothing was wrong. That's the process.

What either side can do during review

  • Walk away. Completely. For any reason, with no penalty.
  • Renegotiate price or terms. Yes, even after signatures.
  • Fix dates, deposit schedules, and inclusions (that chandelier you meant to keep, this is when it gets handled).

That cuts both ways. Your buyer can vanish on day 2 because they found a house they like better. You can also take a stronger backup offer that shows up on day 1. Talk to your attorney before you do anything, but the leverage is real.

What it costs

A typical Bergen County seller pays between $1,750 and $2,250 for an attorney to handle review through closing. Buyers pay in the same range. Against a median Bergen sale price, it's the cheapest insurance in the entire transaction.

Where deals actually die

In my experience it's rarely the lawyers. Deals die in attorney review because one side used the window to keep shopping. The fix is speed: get the signed contract to both attorneys the same day, and push to conclude review fast. Every extra day of open review is a day your deal is optional.

If you're getting ready to sell in Bergen County and want to know what your specific timeline looks like, from offer to review to closing, that's exactly the kind of question I'd rather you ask me before you sign than after.

Common questions

How long is attorney review in New Jersey?

Three business days, starting the day after both parties receive the fully signed contract. Saturdays, Sundays, and legal holidays don't count. It extends automatically if either attorney disapproves the contract and negotiations continue.

Can a seller back out during attorney review in NJ?

Yes. Either side can walk during attorney review for any reason, with no penalty. Once review ends, the contract is binding and backing out gets expensive.

Do I need a lawyer to sell a house in New Jersey?

Legally no, practically yes in North Jersey. Nearly every Bergen County deal uses attorneys on both sides, and a typical seller pays between $1,750 and $2,250 for full representation through closing.
