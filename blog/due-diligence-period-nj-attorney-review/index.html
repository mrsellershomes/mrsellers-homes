Due Diligence Period in New Jersey: Why It Doesn't Exist (You Get Attorney Review Instead)

By Tyler Sellers · July 16, 2026

Last updated July 2026.

If you googled "due diligence period New Jersey" and got ten articles that never quite answer the question, here's why. The term belongs to other states' contracts. New Jersey residential deals don't have a due diligence period. What we have is attorney review, a free 3-business-day escape hatch built into every Realtor-prepared contract, followed by an inspection contingency that's tied to actual defects. I broke down how attorney review works in NJ in a separate post. This one is the comparison, because nobody puts the two side by side and the confusion costs real people real money.

The short version:

  • New Jersey real estate contracts do not have a "due diligence period" or a due diligence fee.
  • Instead, NJ uses a 3-business-day attorney review period, where either side can cancel for any reason, free.
  • After attorney review ends, the inspection contingency lets buyers cancel only over actual defects the inspections find.

Quick disclaimer up front: this is not legal advice. Your contract controls, and your NJ real estate attorney is the one who should read it.

Does New Jersey have a due diligence period?

No. The standard New Jersey residential real estate contract does not have a due diligence period or a due diligence fee. Instead, New Jersey protects both sides through a free, 3-business-day attorney review period, followed by a buyer's inspection contingency. Nothing in the contract gives a buyer a paid, unrestricted right to cancel.

So where did you get the term? National real estate sites. They write one article about "the due diligence period" as if every state works the same way, and New Jersey readers absorb a vocabulary their own contract doesn't use. One of the biggest real estate sites in the country publishes a state-by-state table that lists New Jersey's window at 10 to 14 days. That number doesn't exist in any NJ contract. Attorney review is 3 business days. The 14-day figure they mangled is the inspection window, which is a different protection with different rules.

One wrinkle, so you don't call your attorney a liar. NJ attorneys do use "due diligence" loosely to mean the inspection window. When your NJ lawyer says due diligence, they mean the inspection contingency, and it only lets you out over defects. What New Jersey doesn't have is the due diligence structure: a fee paid to the seller in exchange for an any-reason right to walk. That structure is real. It's just three states south of here.

What does New Jersey have instead of a due diligence period?

Three protections, in sequence. Attorney review, then an inspection contingency, then a mortgage contingency. Each one covers a different risk, and only the first lets you walk for any reason.

Attorney review is the any-reason window. It runs 3 business days from the day after both sides have the fully signed contract, and either side's attorney can reopen or kill the deal during it. Weekends and legal holidays do not count toward the 3 days. No penalty, no fee, no explanation owed. It isn't a courtesy from your agent either. A 1983 NJ Supreme Court consent judgment requires the clause in every Realtor-prepared residential contract in the state. Two things about it that matter for this comparison: it protects both sides equally, and in most NJ deals your main deposit isn't even due until a few days after review concludes. Anything already paid sits in a trust account and comes back in full if the deal dies.

The inspection contingency starts when attorney review ends, typically a 14-day window under the standard form. You inspect, you send the seller your reports, and the seller gets 7 calendar days to agree to fix what you found. Your exit here is tied to defects the inspections actually turn up. It is not a second bite at "any reason."

The mortgage contingency protects you if financing falls through. Your contract sets a commitment deadline, typically 30 to 45 days, and if you made a good-faith effort and the loan dies anyway, you exit with your deposit.

That's the whole NJ architecture. Free any-reason window first, then two narrow, condition-tied exits. Check the dates in your own contract, because all of these can be negotiated.

What is a due diligence period in North Carolina, Georgia, and Texas?

It's a negotiated window where the buyer purchases the right to cancel for any reason. In North Carolina the buyer pays the seller a non-refundable due diligence fee for that right. Texas calls it an option period and the fee is smaller. Georgia skips the fee but makes the deadline unforgiving.

North Carolina is the vivid one. The due diligence fee goes straight to the seller, and the seller keeps it no matter what happens. Walk away on day 2, seller keeps it. Find a cracked foundation, seller keeps it. It only gets credited toward the price if you actually close. Typical fees run $500 to $2,000. In the 2021 and 2022 frenzy, buyers on competitive Charlotte and Raleigh listings were reportedly paying $10,000 to $50,000, non-refundable, just to hold a two-week look at the house.

Texas runs the same idea at lower stakes. The buyer pays an option fee, typically $100 to $500, for an unrestricted right to terminate during the option period. Seller keeps the fee, and it's credited at closing if the deal closes.

Georgia sits in between. No separate fee, and earnest money comes back if the buyer's written termination lands before the deadline. Miss that deadline by a day and the earnest money is typically gone.

Now read New Jersey against that. Here, the right to walk away from a signed contract is free. It's built into the contract by court order, it protects the seller just as much as the buyer, and in most deals the deposit hasn't even changed hands yet. In North Carolina, buyers pay the seller real money for a weaker version of that same right. That's the whole reason this comparison is worth a page.

What's the difference between attorney review and a due diligence period?

Who it protects and what it costs. Attorney review protects both sides and costs nothing. A due diligence period protects only the buyer, and in the fee states the buyer pays the seller for it.

Can you back out during the NJ inspection period for any reason?

No, and this is where buyers trained on national articles get burned. Once attorney review ends, the contract is binding. The inspection contingency only covers defects your inspections actually find, and the seller gets a chance to cure, meaning offer repairs or a credit, before you can walk.

So if you're having second thoughts about the house itself, the price, the commute, any of it, attorney review is your window. Say so then, while leaving is free. Trying to manufacture an exit through inspections later is how deposits end up in dispute. In practice most inspection issues don't kill deals anyway. Sellers usually respond with a credit, commonly $2,000 to $5,000 on the deals I see, and I covered where that lands in the seller's math in my Bergen County seller closing costs breakdown.

Sellers, same lesson inverted. A buyer who's still shopping will show it during review, not after. Get the signed contract to both attorneys the same day and push to wrap review quickly. A deal sitting in open review is still a maybe.

Common questions

Does New Jersey have a due diligence period?

No. NJ contracts use attorney review, which runs 3 business days and lets either side walk away for free, plus an inspection contingency tied to defects. There is no due diligence fee and no any-reason walk-away window after attorney review ends.

What's the difference between attorney review and a due diligence period?

Attorney review is free and protects both buyer and seller for 3 business days after signing. A due diligence period in states like North Carolina, Georgia, and Texas is a buyer-only right the buyer pays for. In North Carolina the fee is non-refundable and the seller keeps it even if the buyer walks.

Does New Jersey have a due diligence fee?

No. Nothing in the NJ standard contract requires paying the seller for the right to cancel. Your deposit is held in trust and returned in full if the deal dies during attorney review.

Can you back out during the NJ inspection period for any reason?

No. After attorney review ends, the inspection contingency only covers defects your inspections turn up, and the seller gets a chance to fix them first. The any-reason window in New Jersey is attorney review. Use it.
