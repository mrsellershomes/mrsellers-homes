Bergen County Seller Closing Costs 2026: Every Line Item

By Tyler Sellers · July 13, 2026

Last updated July 2026.

I sell homes in Bergen County. Before every listing appointment I walk sellers through what they will actually net, line by line, because almost nobody has seen these numbers laid out honestly. Most articles about NJ closing costs are statewide filler with wrong math. This page is the opposite: every line item, real dollar figures observed on my own closings, and enough detail that you can calculate your own number before you list.

A quick disclaimer for full transparency: this is not legal or tax advice. Confirm your numbers with your attorney and CPA. The fees below were observed on real closings; yours will vary.

How much are seller closing costs in Bergen County, NJ in 2026?

Plan on roughly 6% to 6.5% of your sale price, before your mortgage payoff. The formula: commission around 5%, plus the NJ Realty Transfer Fee at about 0.9%, plus an attorney around $2,000, plus $500 to $800 in municipal and title odds and ends. Every piece is knowable in advance.

On a $788,000 sale, Bergen County's median as of the three months ending May 2026 per Redfin, that is about $49,100 to $49,700. The full worked example is at the bottom of this page.

What does a seller pay at closing in NJ besides commission?

Six buckets. Commission, the Realty Transfer Fee, your attorney, municipal items, small title-side fees, and then your mortgage payoff plus prorations. The first five are costs. The last one is just settling up.

A few terms, defined once so the rest of this page reads clean:

  • ALTA statement: the itemized settlement sheet the title company prepares at closing. It lists every dollar in and out for both sides. The same closing can also generate a Closing Disclosure and a HUD-style statement that label identical dollars differently. Confusing by design, basically. The ALTA is the one I work from.
  • RTF (Realty Transfer Fee): New Jersey's state fee for recording your deed. More below. It is the one line most sellers have never heard of.
  • Payoff: the exact amount needed to pay off your mortgage on closing day, including interest through that date. Biggest number on every statement, but it is your own debt, not a cost of selling.
  • Proration: splitting a bill by days of ownership. Property taxes are the big one. On every one of my recent closings the proration ran in the seller's favor, because the seller had prepaid the quarter and the buyer reimbursed the unused days.

One modern line item older guides miss: if you have leased or financed solar panels, expect a UCC lien discharge fee at closing, around $75, plus whatever your solar agreement requires to transfer or pay off.

What is the NJ Realty Transfer Fee and who pays it?

The seller pays it. It is a state fee (it does not vary by town, despite what some websites claim), and it is exactly calculable before you list. The county will not record your deed until it is paid.

The RTF is not a flat percentage. It stacks brackets, like income tax, in units of $500 of sale price. Here is the standard 2026 schedule for sales over $350,000:

Slice of the priceRate per $500
$0 to $150,000$2.90
$150,001 to $200,000$4.25
$200,001 to $550,000$4.80
$550,001 to $850,000$5.30
$850,001 to $1,000,000$5.80
Over $1,000,000$6.05

(Sales at $350,000 or under use a cheaper schedule: $2.00, $3.35, and $3.90 per $500 across similar slices.)

At Bergen prices this works out to roughly 0.9% of the sale price. I checked this schedule against actual settlement statements from my own closings and it matched exactly, to the penny. On a recent $621,000 Bergen County sale the schedule computes $5,407.60, and $5,407.60 is what the ALTA shows. Same story at $565,000 ($4,814.00) and $820,000 ($7,517.00).

Do the math for your own price: divide each slice of your price by 500, multiply by that slice's rate, add them up. Or ask me and I will run it for you in about a minute.

Do seniors get a discount on the NJ Realty Transfer Fee?

Yes, and it is big: roughly half. New Jersey has a partial exemption for qualifying sellers who are 62 or older, blind, or disabled (low- and moderate-income housing also qualifies). For sales over $350,000, the discounted rates run $1.40 to $3.40 per $500 instead of $2.90 to $6.05.

The basic requirements for eligibility: you must own AND occupy the home as your residence, be a New Jersey resident, and the property must be a one- or two-family home. Joint owners generally must all qualify, though for married couples one qualifying spouse is enough. It is a personal exemption, so a trust never qualifies. You claim it with an Affidavit of Consideration (form RTF-1) filed with the deed. Confirm your specific eligibility with your attorney.

Now the trap. Never estimate your RTF by copying the number off a neighbor's or a prior sale's settlement statement. Their statement may quietly include this exemption. I saw a $735,000 Bergen County closing where the RTF was $3,120.50. The standard schedule says $6,616.00. The seller qualified for the senior exemption. Copy that number without qualifying and you are $3,500 short at the closing table. Always recompute from the schedule for the specific seller.

Who pays the NJ mansion tax in 2026?

The seller, since July 10, 2025. This is a real change and a lot of sellers have not heard about it. The old rule had the buyer paying a flat 1% "mansion tax" on sales over $1 million. The 2025 law flipped it to the seller and made it graduated:

Sale priceRate on the ENTIRE price
Over $1M to $2M1%
Over $2M to $2.5M2%
Over $2.5M to $3M2.5%
Over $3M to $3.5M3%
Over $3.5M3.5%

Read that middle column again. The rate applies to the whole price, not just the amount over the threshold. This creates cliffs. Sell at $1,000,000 and you owe zero. Sell at $1,000,001 and you owe about $10,000. Sell at $2,000,001 instead of $2,000,000 and the jump is more than $20,000. And this fee stacks on top of the standard Realty Transfer Fee.

With so many Bergen County homes trading above $1 million now, pricing carefully around these thresholds is a vital listing strategy conversation. Have it before you list, not at closing.

Is there really a New Jersey exit tax?

No. There is no separate tax for leaving New Jersey. The "exit tax" is one of the most repeated myths in NJ real estate, and it costs sellers real anxiety every year.

Here is what actually happens. If you sell and you are moving out of state, New Jersey requires an estimated income tax prepayment withheld at closing. It is a deposit against the income tax you may owe on your gain, not an extra tax. You reconcile it when you file your NJ return, and if you owe less than what was withheld, you get the difference refunded. Many sellers get most or all of it back, especially when the primary-residence capital gains exclusion wipes out the taxable gain.

If you are staying in New Jersey, you file form GIT/REP-3 at closing and pay nothing extra at the table.

The state's logic is simple: once you move to Florida, New Jersey loses its easy way to collect, so it takes a deposit up front. Annoying, yes. A special exit tax, no. Your CPA can tell you what your withholding and likely refund look like.

Common questions

Who pays the Realty Transfer Fee in New Jersey, buyer or seller?

The seller. New Jersey law makes the seller responsible for the Realty Transfer Fee, and the deed cannot be recorded until it is paid. The parties can negotiate who actually funds it, but the seller stays on the hook if it is underpaid.

Does the seller pay title insurance in New Jersey?

No. In standard New Jersey practice the buyer pays for the owner's title insurance policy. Some websites claim otherwise. On real Bergen County settlement statements, the seller's title-related charges are small items like release recording and wire fees, usually a few hundred dollars total.

How much is the NJ Realty Transfer Fee on a $600,000 house?

$5,185 on the standard schedule. That is 300 units at $2.90, plus 100 at $4.25, plus 700 at $4.80, plus 100 at $5.30, where each unit is $500 of price. Sellers who qualify for the senior or disabled partial exemption pay roughly half.

Is the NJ mansion tax charged on the whole price or just the amount over $1 million?

The whole price. Since July 10, 2025 the seller pays a graduated fee of 1% to 3.5% on the entire sale price once it crosses $1 million. Selling at $1,000,001 instead of $1,000,000 costs the seller about $10,000 more.
