About Tyler Sellers

Not your typical
Bergen County
real estate agent.

I grew up here. I left. I worked in tech, tried to build something abroad, came home, and stumbled into real estate on a drive down Main Street. That unconventional path is exactly why I’m better at this job than most people who planned for it.

The full story

I was born and raised in Bergen County. At 14, I earned a spot at Regis High School in Manhattan — one of the most academically rigorous schools in the country. For four years I commuted into NYC every day, which gave me something most Bergen County kids don’t get: an early, firsthand understanding of exactly what the NYC-to-suburbs commute looks and feels like. That knowledge still informs how I advise buyers today.

After Regis I went to Santa Clara University in California, where I studied finance. After graduating in 2016 I moved to San Francisco to work at Salesforce on their recruiting team. I was on track toward sales, but I had always wanted to build something of my own. So in 2018 I took a risk — I left tech, and my brother and I moved to Southeast Asia to build an online fitness company for guys who wanted to build muscle and improve their lives.

We set up in Chiang Mai, Thailand first, then found a community of entrepreneurs and expats and moved into a villa in Canggu, Bali. The business didn’t scale the way we hoped — we were years ahead of a trend that would explode during the pandemic — but I learned more about content creation, online marketing, and building an audience from scratch than any job could have taught me.

I came home to Bergen County in mid-2019 to regroup. While I figured out my next move, I went back to work with my father — a wallpaper installer who has spent 44+ years working in the most prestigious homes in Bergen County. Alpine, Tenafly, Saddle River, Ridgewood. I had been working alongside him since I was 11 years old. I know what premium craftsmanship looks like because I’ve been inside those homes most agents have only seen in listings.

I also worked at a home decorating center, installing window shades and blinds in the high-rise towers along the Fort Lee palisades and in residential homes across Bergen County. Between my father’s work and mine, I built an eye for construction quality, materials, and the kind of details that don’t show up in listing photos but absolutely affect value.

One day I was driving down Main Street in Ridgefield Park and saw a sign: “Real estate salespeople wanted.” I was heading into sales in NYC anyway. But this one was right in my backyard. I met the broker, Ted Crocco, who informed me I needed a license first — handed me a $50 coupon to real estate school — and the rest followed quickly. I enrolled at Kovats Real Estate School, one of the most respected programs in New Jersey, took my state exam the day after finishing class, and passed.

I started out on a small team, sold seven homes, and learned the business from the ground up. Real estate is nothing like tech. The procedures exist, but only as outlines. Every transaction is different. Every client is different. You figure it out as you go, and the agents who do it best are the ones who genuinely care about the outcome.

In 2021 I went solo and turned to social media to build my own client base. My Regis and college connections weren’t in Bergen County — I had to create my own storefront. I started making content about Bergen County homes, towns, and the real estate market. Today I have over 15,000 followers across TikTok, Instagram, and YouTube, and I sell about seven to ten homes a year with a goal of reaching 12–15. I’m at RE/MAX because they have the best technology and the fastest response to changes in New Jersey real estate law — which matters when you’re acting as a fiduciary for people making the biggest financial decision of their lives.

The Regis education instilled something in me that has never left: the belief that I am a man for others. I have moved truckloads of furniture. I have painted rooms. I have installed railings. I have answered calls on vacation. I have negotiated $15,000 under asking in a market where that doesn’t happen. Not because I had to, but because that’s what the job actually means to me.
